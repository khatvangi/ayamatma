## The Metaphysical Question

How does one recognize Krishna as <Term iast="paramātman">Paramātman</Term>? This is a metaphysical question with an epistemological answer. It asks: by what means does a human being move from seeing a form, a historical presence, a remarkable person, to recognizing the Supreme Reality shining through and as that presence?

In a Vedāntic frame, the answer cannot rest on wonder, charisma, miracle, power, or emotional intensity. It must rest on knowledge. The thesis of this essay: the <Term iast="udyoga parva">Udyoga Parva's</Term> <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term>-<Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> dialogue (Mbh 5.67, BORI CE) states this with precision. Brahman-in-form is recognized through <Term iast="ātma vidyā">Ātma Vidyā</Term>, direct Upaniṣadic self-knowledge, and through nothing else. By "Brahman-in-form" I mean the nondual reality (<Term iast="nirguṇa brahman">nirguṇa Brahman</Term>) as it becomes accessible through a particular embodied locus, recognized not by the form's qualities but by the knower's <Term iast="vidyā">vidyā</Term>.

## Why Brahman Cannot Be Known by Ordinary Means

The <Term iast="kena upaniṣad">Kena Upaniṣad</Term> establishes the epistemological problem at the outset. <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term> is the ground of cognition, not its content:

> यन्मनसा न मनुते येनाहुर्मनो मतम्
> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="kena-1-6">*yan manasā na manute yenāhur mano matam*</span> (Kena 1.6)
> "That which the mind does not think, by which, they say, the mind is thought."

Recognition of <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term> cannot work like recognition of any other thing. Seeing extraordinary qualities, verifying miracles, inferring from impressive effects, social consensus, devotional intensity: none of these reach <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term>, because <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term> is what makes seeing, verifying, inferring, and feeling possible in the first place.

The <Term iast="muṇḍaka upaniṣad">Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad</Term> sharpens the point by distinguishing two orders of knowledge (1.1.4-5): <Term iast="apara-vidyā">Apara-Vidyā</Term>, which covers the Vedas as ritual, grammar, metrics, astronomy, and all empirical sciences; and <Term iast="para-vidyā">Para-Vidyā</Term>, by which the Imperishable (*<Term iast="akṣara">akṣara</Term>*) is known. The distinction is structural, not a ranking of better and worse study habits. <Term iast="apara-vidyā">Apara-Vidyā</Term> operates within <Term iast="māyā">Māyā's</Term> domain. A mind confined to it cannot recognize <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term> regardless of what stands before it, because it is looking at the surface of the mirror rather than what the mirror discloses.

The <Term iast="muṇḍaka upaniṣad">Muṇḍaka</Term> specifies the conditions under which recognition becomes possible:

> यमेवैष वृणुते तेन लभ्यस्तस्यैष आत्मा विवृणुते तनूँ स्वाम्
> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="mundaka-3-2-3">*yam evaiṣa vṛṇute tena labhyas tasyaiṣa ātmā vivṛṇute tanūṃ svām*</span> (Muṇ. 3.2.3)
> "Whom this [ātman] chooses, by him alone is it obtained; to him this ātman reveals its own form."

The verb *vṛṇute* (chooses/selects) carries a real ambiguity the Vedāntic tradition has debated. In a theistic reading (Rāmānuja, for instance), this is grace: the Lord chooses the devotee. In the Advaitic reading, it is reflexive: the <Term iast="ātman">ātman</Term> "chooses" the one whose instrument is already aligned with it, meaning the preparation *is* the condition of disclosure. This essay follows the Advaitic reading, but the tension is genuine and should not be smoothed over. Both readings agree on the negative point: accumulation of external evidence does not produce recognition.

The <Term iast="kaṭha upaniṣad">Kaṭha Upaniṣad</Term> confirms the exclusion:

> नायमात्मा प्रवचनेन लभ्यो न मेधया न बहुना श्रुतेन
> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="katha-1-2-23">*nāyam ātmā pravacanena labhyo na medhayā na bahunā śrutena*</span> (Kaṭha 1.2.23)
> "This ātman is not obtained by instruction, nor by intellect, nor by extensive learning."

The exclusion of *pravacana* (discourse), *medhā* (intellect), and *bahu-śruta* (scriptural accumulation) as sufficient conditions leaves only one thing: the orientation of the knower. That orientation is <Term iast="ātma vidyā">Ātma Vidyā</Term>.

This is the Vedāntic frame within which <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> answer operates.

## Sañjaya's Answer: Three Conditions, One Architecture

<Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> is physically blind. He is also the <Term iast="udyoga parva">Udyoga Parva's</Term> figure for consciousness oriented entirely toward <Term iast="sṛṣṭi">Sṛṣṭi</Term>: toward kingdom, succession, family, power, the preservation of what he holds. He asks <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term>:

> कथं त्वं माधवं वेत्थ सर्वलोकमहेश्वरम् ।
> कथमेनं न वेदाहं तन्ममाचक्ष्व सञ्जय ॥
> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="mbh-5-67-1">*kathaṃ tvaṃ mādhavaṃ vettha sarvalokamaheśvaram | katham enaṃ na vedāhaṃ tan mamācakṣva sañjaya*</span> (Mbh 5.67.1, BORI CE)
> "How do you know Mādhava as the Lord of all worlds? How is it that I do not know him? Tell me this, Sañjaya."

The question is the text's own diagnostic of two fundamentally different cognitive orientations.

<Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> answers first with the overarching claim:

> विद्या राजन्न ते विद्या मम विद्या न हीयते ।
> विद्याहीनस्तमोध्वस्तो नाभिजानाति केशवम् ॥
> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="mbh-5-67-2">*vidyā rājanna te vidyā mama vidyā na hīyate | vidyāhīnas tamodhvasto nābhijānāti keśavam*</span> (Mbh 5.67.2)
> "O King, you have no <Term iast="vidyā">vidyā</Term>. My <Term iast="vidyā">vidyā</Term> does not diminish. One bereft of <Term iast="vidyā">vidyā</Term>, engulfed in darkness, does not recognize Keśava."

> विद्यया तात जानामि त्रियुगं मधुसूदनम् ।
> कर्तारमकृतं देवं भूतानां प्रभवाप्ययम् ॥
> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="mbh-5-67-3">*vidyayā tāta jānāmi triyugaṃ madhusūdanam | kartāram akṛtaṃ devaṃ bhūtānāṃ prabhavāpyayam*</span> (Mbh 5.67.3)
> "By <Term iast="vidyā">vidyā</Term>, father, I know Madhusūdana, the three-aged, the maker who is unmade, the origin and dissolution of beings."

<span class="pullquote">The BORI Sanskrit says vidyayā without the prefix "ātma," but the content makes the identification clear: the vidyā in question is the knowledge by which one recognizes the kartāram akṛtaṃ devam, the uncreated creator.</span>

<Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> then asks the follow-up: what is the nature of your constant <Term iast="bhakti">bhakti</Term> toward Janārdana? (5.67.4). <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> answer is the essay's central text:

> मायां न सेवे भद्रं ते न वृथाधर्ममाचरे ।
> शुद्धभावं गतो भक्त्या शास्त्राद्वेद्मि जनार्दनम् ॥
> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="mbh-5-67-5">*māyāṃ na seve bhadraṃ te na vṛthādharmam ācare | śuddhabhāvaṃ gato bhaktyā śāstrād vedmi janārdanam*</span> (Mbh 5.67.5)
> "I do not serve <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>, blessings upon you. I do not practise <Term iast="vyartha">vṛthā-dharma</Term> [purposeless dharma]. Having attained purity of being (<Term iast="śuddha-bhāva">śuddha-bhāva</Term>), through <Term iast="bhakti">bhakti</Term> and <Term iast="śāstra">śāstra</Term> I know Janārdana."

The three conditions are stated in a single śloka. They describe a unified cognitive architecture, not a sequence of stages.

### First Condition: Non-Orientation Toward Māyā

*māyāṃ na seve* — "I do not serve <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>."

<Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> does not claim to have overcome <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>. He says he does not worship it, does not orient his cognitive attention toward <Term iast="sṛṣṭi">Sṛṣṭi</Term> as the primary locus of reality. The <Term iast="bhagavad gītā">Bhagavad Gītā</Term> states the cosmological structure:

> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="bg-7-14">*daivī hy eṣā guṇamayī mama māyā duratyayā / mām eva ye prapadyante māyām etāṃ taranti te*</span> (BG 7.14)
> "This divine <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term> of mine, constituted of the <Term iast="guṇa">guṇas</Term>, is hard to cross; those who take refuge in me alone cross over this <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>."

The operative phrase is *mām eva*, "in me alone," pointing toward the substratum rather than the contents. <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term> here carries its full Vedāntic weight: the superimposition of multiplicity and change onto what is one and unchanging. The <Term iast="guṇa">guṇa</Term>-bound empirical world treated as self-grounded is <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>. This includes the obvious worldly attachments and the subtler ones: personality cults, spiritual theater, novelty of experience, the transient phenomena the discourse names.

A clarification is necessary here. <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> rejection of <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term> is a rejection of cognitive orientation toward the impermanent-as-ultimate. It is not a rejection of devotional form. The worship of Śiva, Gaṇeśa, or Devī, understood as Brahman-appearing-as-form (<Term iast="saguṇa brahman">Saguna Brahman</Term>), is a different act entirely from <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra's</Term> worship of his sons' kingdom. The Ṛgveda states the structure: <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="rv-1-164-46">*ekam sad viprā bahudhā vadanti*</span> (RV 1.164.46) — "The Real is one; the wise speak of it variously." Krishna confirms this in the <Term iast="gītā">Gītā</Term>: whatever form a devotee worships with <Term iast="śraddhā">śraddhā</Term>, he makes that faith steadfast (BG 7.21). The problem is not form. The problem is orientation. Worshiping anything with the ego's attachment and without liberation as the telos is <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>-worship, regardless of how the object is labeled.

The <Term iast="kaṭha upaniṣad">Kaṭha Upaniṣad</Term> frames the same point as a psychological choice:

> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="katha-1-2-2">*śreyas ca preyas ca manuṣyam etas tau samparītya vivinakti dhīraḥ*</span> (Kaṭha 1.2.2)
> "The good (<Term iast="śreyas">śreyas</Term>) and the pleasant (<Term iast="preyas">preyas</Term>) both approach a person; the wise, having examined both, distinguishes them."

BG 7.14 states the cosmological structure; Kaṭha 1.2.2 states the psychological choice. They describe the same axis. <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> non-orientation toward <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term> is this choice, practised continuously as cognitive discipline rather than invoked as metaphysical classification.

Rejecting <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term> is ontological sobriety: refusing to absolutize the relative.

### Second Condition: Dharma Anchored in Brahman-Realization

*na vṛthādharmam ācare* — "I do not practise <Term iast="vyartha">vṛthā-dharma</Term> [purposeless dharma]."

<Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> refuses <Term iast="dharma">Dharma</Term> performed for social function, familial obligation, or karmic accumulation alone. <Term iast="dharma">Dharma</Term> without the telos of <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term>-realization he identifies as *<Term iast="vyartha">vyartha</Term>*, empty. The <Term iast="gītā">Gītā's</Term> canonical statement of the corrective:

> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="bg-9-27">*yat karoṣi yad aśnāsi yaj juhoṣi dadāsi yat / yat tapasyasi kaunteya tat kuruṣva mad-arpaṇam*</span> (BG 9.27)
> "Whatever you do, whatever you eat, whatever you offer, whatever you give, whatever austerity you perform, O Kaunteya, do that as an offering unto Me."

This dissolves formalism. Action by itself does not liberate, because <Term iast="mokṣa">mokṣa</Term> is not a product of action. The <Term iast="muṇḍaka upaniṣad">Muṇḍaka</Term> states the structural limit:

> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="mundaka-1-2-12">*parīkṣya lokān karmacitān brāhmaṇo nirvedam āyān nāstyakṛtaḥ kṛtena*</span> (Muṇ. 1.2.12)
> "Having examined worlds won by action, a brahmin should arrive at non-attachment: what is not made cannot be reached by what is made."

Action-as-such (*kṛta*) cannot reach the unconditioned (*akṛta*). What <Term iast="karma-yoga">karma-yoga</Term> produces is <Term iast="antaḥkaraṇa-śuddhi">antaḥkaraṇa-śuddhi</Term>, purification of the inner instrument, which prepares the mind for <Term iast="śravaṇa">śravaṇa</Term>, <Term iast="manana">manana</Term>, and <Term iast="nididhyāsana">nididhyāsana</Term>. <Term iast="karma-mārga">Karma-mārga</Term> requires <Term iast="jñāna-mārga">jñāna-mārga</Term> as its telos, or it recycles indefinitely. <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> refusal of *vyartha* dharma is the refusal of this recycling.

### Third Condition: The Unified Sādhana

*śuddhabhāvaṃ gato bhaktyā śāstrād vedmi janārdanam* — "Having attained purity of being, through <Term iast="bhakti">bhakti</Term> and <Term iast="śāstra">śāstra</Term> I know Janārdana."

<Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> names three elements operating together: <Term iast="śuddha manas">Śuddha Manas</Term>, <Term iast="bhakti">Bhakti</Term>, and <Term iast="śāstra">Śāstra</Term>. They constitute a single cognitive mode.

<span class="pullquote">Separated from jñāna, bhakti degenerates into personality-cult. Separated from bhakti, jñāna becomes inert. Separated from śāstra, both become projection.</span>

**<Term iast="śuddha manas">Śuddha Manas</Term>** (purified mind) is the absence of the distortions that make Brahman-recognition structurally impossible: craving, aversion, and the misidentification of <Term iast="ātman">ātman</Term> with body-mind. Krishna's description at BG 10.11 frames the relationship: *<Term iast="jñāna-dīpena bhāsvatā">jñāna-dīpena bhāsvatā</Term>*, "with the shining lamp of knowledge." <Term iast="śuddha manas">Śuddha Manas</Term> is the medium through which the lamp can shine. A turbid medium cannot receive the light.

The <Term iast="gītā">Gītā</Term> illuminates the mechanism by which a purified mind becomes capable of receiving knowledge. The operative concept is *<Term iast="śraddhā">śraddhā</Term>*, which <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> does not name explicitly but which is structurally implicit in his triad. <Term iast="śaṅkara">Śaṅkara's</Term> <Term iast="vivekacūḍāmaṇi">Vivekacūḍāmaṇi</Term> (25) defines it: *śāstrasya guru-vākyasya satya-buddhy-avadhāraṇam*, the firm conviction that the words of scripture and teacher are true. (This is <Term iast="śaṅkara">Śaṅkara's</Term> definition, not the <Term iast="gītā">Gītā's</Term> own; the distinction matters.) The <Term iast="gītā">Gītā</Term> gives both the positive and negative case:

> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="bg-4-39">*śraddhāvān labhate jñānam tatparaḥ saṃyatendriyaḥ*</span> (BG 4.39)
> "The one of firm conviction, intent upon it, sense-controlled, attains knowledge."

> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="bg-4-40">*ajñaś cāśraddadhānaś ca saṃśayātmā vinaśyati*</span> (BG 4.40)
> "The ignorant, the faithless, and the doubting self perish."

The sequence in 4.39 is exact: <Term iast="śraddhā">śraddhā</Term> enables <Term iast="jñāna">jñāna</Term>. A <Term iast="śuddha manas">śuddha manas</Term> that engages <Term iast="śāstra">śāstra</Term> with <Term iast="bhakti">bhakti</Term> already operates with <Term iast="śraddhā">śraddhā</Term>, the epistemological posture that holds <Term iast="śabda-pramāṇa">śabda-pramāṇa</Term> (testimony) as authoritative for what lies beyond perception and inference. When <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> looks at Krishna, he sees <Term iast="tattva">Tattva</Term> rather than a cousin or a diplomat. This is the recognition, toward which Kena 1.4-9 points, that <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term> is unavailable to the instrument that refuses testimony and insists on empirical verification alone.

**<Term iast="bhakti">Bhakti</Term>**, in <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> usage, is cognitive adhesion to <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term>. The <Term iast="bṛhadāraṇyaka upaniṣad">Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad</Term> formulates it as <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="brh-1-4-15">*ātmānam eva lokam upāsīta*</span> (BU 1.4.15), "let one worship the self alone as one's world." The <Term iast="gītā">Gītā's</Term> highest statement on <Term iast="bhakti">bhakti</Term> makes its epistemological function explicit:

> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="bg-18-55">*bhaktyā mām abhijānāti yāvān yaś cāsmi tattvataḥ / tato māṃ tattvato jñātvā viśate tad anantaram*</span> (BG 18.55)
> "Through devotion one knows me truly, what I am and who I am in reality. Having known me truly, one enters me thereupon."

The verb is *abhijānāti*, from √jñā with the prefix *abhi*: direct cognitive apprehension, not inference or sensory perception. In the Advaitic reading, <Term iast="bhakti">bhakti</Term> functions here as the medium of <Term iast="jñāna">jñāna</Term>, not its substitute. Rāmānuja reads the same verse differently: <Term iast="bhakti">bhakti</Term> has independent soteriological power, and <Term iast="jñāna">jñāna</Term> is its product rather than its master. This essay follows the Advaitic reading because it coheres with <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> own framing ("I know through <Term iast="ātma vidyā">Ātma Vidyā</Term>"), but the disagreement between these two readings is real, not a matter of emphasis.

**<Term iast="śāstra">Śāstra</Term>** is the corrective that prevents <Term iast="bhakti">bhakti</Term> from becoming projection. The <Term iast="chāndogya upaniṣad">Chāndogya's</Term> *ācāryavān puruṣo veda* (ChU 6.14.2), "a person with a teacher knows," establishes that <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term>-knowledge requires śāstric transmission. Scripture functions as <Term iast="pramāṇa">pramāṇa</Term>, a valid means of knowledge for what neither perception nor inference can reach. Without <Term iast="śāstra">śāstra</Term>, the <Term iast="bhakta">bhakta</Term> constructs a personal <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term> in the image of his own psychology. With <Term iast="śāstra">śāstra</Term> rightly heard, the <Term iast="mahāvākya">mahāvākyas</Term> — *tat tvam asi* (ChU 6.8.7) and *ahaṃ brahmāsmi* (BU 1.4.10) — function as what they are: precise teaching sentences that correct the seeker's fundamental misidentification.

## The Mechanism: Jñāna as Primary Pramāṇa

<Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> recognition of Krishna as <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term> is a <Term iast="jñāna">jñāna</Term>-event. The three conditions describe the same reorientation from three directions: correct ontological orientation (non-<Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>), correct teleological orientation (<Term iast="dharma">Dharma</Term> with telos), and correct epistemic instrument (unified sādhana). This is worth stating plainly: the three conditions are not really three. They are one orientation seen from different angles. A mind that does not serve <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>, that refuses purposeless <Term iast="dharma">dharma</Term>, and that operates with <Term iast="śuddha manas">śuddha manas</Term>, <Term iast="bhakti">bhakti</Term>, and <Term iast="śāstra">śāstra</Term> is one mind, configured in one way. The tripartite presentation is expository, not ontological.

When these converge, the mechanism described in Muṇḍaka 3.2.3 operates: the <Term iast="ātman">ātman</Term> discloses its own form to the prepared instrument. Recognition follows. It cannot be manufactured by other means.

<span class="pullquote">A mind oriented toward Māyā cannot recognize Brahman even when Brahman stands directly in front of it, as Dhritarāṣṭra demonstrates perfectly.</span>

This is <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra's</Term> impossibility stated precisely. His cognitive architecture is oriented toward <Term iast="sṛṣṭi">Sṛṣṭi</Term> at every level: his <Term iast="dharma">Dharma</Term> is the preservation of his sons' kingdom, his attachment is to <Term iast="māyā">Māyā's</Term> products, his instrument is uncorrected by <Term iast="ātma vidyā">Ātma Vidyā</Term>. Read in the context of the <Term iast="udyoga parva">Udyoga Parva</Term>, Kaṭha 1.2.23 functions as <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra's</Term> diagnosis. He has *pravacana*: he has been instructed. He has *medhā*: he is not intellectually deficient. He has *bahu-śruta*: he has heard scripture. None of these suffice, because the orientation itself is wrong.

## Architectonic Grounding: The Sanatsujāta Placement

The <Term iast="udyoga parva">Udyoga Parva</Term> places the <Term iast="sanatsujāta">Sanatsujāta</Term> section (UP 83-88) immediately before <Term iast="bhagavad yāna">Bhagavad Yāna</Term> (Krishna's embassy, UP 68-69). <Term iast="vidura">Vidura</Term> brings <Term iast="sanatsujāta">Sanatsujāta</Term> to instruct <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> on <Term iast="brahma vidyā">Brahma Vidyā</Term> and <Term iast="ātman">ātman</Term>. The <Term iast="sanatsujāta parva">Sanatsujāta Parva</Term> is among the <Term iast="mahābhārata">Mahābhārata's</Term> most concentrated expositions of Upaniṣadic self-knowledge.

<span class="pullquote">Scripture is heard; Vidyā is absent. The manas receives information and fails to undergo transformation.</span>

The structural argument: <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> receives the highest Vedāntic instruction. He cannot act on it. Then Krishna arrives. <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> cannot recognize him. In the received structure of the <Term iast="udyoga parva">Udyoga Parva</Term>, this sequence creates a powerful interpretive frame: the reader understands <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra's</Term> failure before Krishna appears. Whether or not this reflects deliberate compositional design (epic redactional layering makes that claim difficult to secure), the literary function is clear. Knowledge received without integration remains information, not <Term iast="vidyā">vidyā</Term>. The <Term iast="udyoga parva">Udyoga Parva</Term> demonstrates this structurally, not by assertion.

<Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> position in the same text confirms the other half. He is <Term iast="vyāsa">Vyāsa's</Term> disciple. The *<Term iast="divya cakṣus">divya cakṣus</Term>* <Term iast="vyāsa">Vyāsa</Term> grants him is the tradition's idiom for the perceptual transformation that <Term iast="brahma vidyā">Brahma Vidyā</Term> produces. <Term iast="vyāsa">Vyāsa</Term> grants <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> *<Term iast="sarvajñatā">sarvajñatā</Term>*, complete cognition of the war. But <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> recognition of Krishna as <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term> precedes this grant. He can report the war truly because he already sees truly. The epistemology is prior to the narration.

## The Knower Changes

Recognition of Krishna as <Term iast="paramātman">Paramātman</Term> is not a conclusion *about* Krishna. It is a transformation *of the knower*.

The obstacle to recognition is not lack of external information. <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> has information; he has been told exactly who Krishna is by multiple sources, including <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> himself. The obstacle is *<Term iast="avidyā">avidyā</Term>*: the misidentification of <Term iast="ātman">ātman</Term> with body-mind that keeps the cognitive instrument oriented toward <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>. <Term iast="avidyā">Avidyā</Term> is structural, not factual. Its cure is not more facts but the transformation of the instrument through <Term iast="ātma vidyā">Ātma Vidyā</Term>.

This is why the Upaniṣads continuously redirect inward. *Tat tvam asi* (ChU 6.8.7) is not a claim about Krishna's external qualities or an inference from his deeds. It is the correction of a misidentification. *Ahaṃ brahmāsmi* (BU 1.4.10) is the culmination of a process in which the knower's self-understanding has been altered by Vedāntic inquiry. When <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> recognizes Krishna as <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term>, what has happened is that <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> *<Term iast="manas">manas</Term>*, shaped by <Term iast="ātma vidyā">Ātma Vidyā</Term>, has ceased to misidentify the ground of reality as something other than itself.

The three-mārga synthesis follows from this. <Term iast="karma-mārga">Karma-mārga</Term> produces <Term iast="antaḥkaraṇa-śuddhi">antaḥkaraṇa-śuddhi</Term>, the inner purification without which recognition is impossible. <Term iast="jñāna-mārga">Jñāna-mārga</Term> produces the actual recognition, the <Term iast="ātman">ātman</Term>-<Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term> identity disclosed through Vedāntic inquiry. <Term iast="bhakti-mārga">Bhakti-mārga</Term> sustains the orientation that keeps the instrument aligned with <Term iast="para-vidyā">Para-Vidyā</Term> rather than <Term iast="apara-vidyā">Apara-Vidyā</Term>. These are not three paths between which a seeker chooses based on temperament. They are three functions that must operate together for the knower to change.

## What This Means Now

The <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term>-<Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> exchange poses a direct challenge to contemporary Vedāntic culture. "How do you recognize Brahman-in-form?" is posed to every student who approaches a teacher claiming to embody <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term>. Contemporary answers almost never resemble <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term>.

They run: *I recognized him through his miracles. Through the peace in his presence. Through thousands of others who recognized him. Through prayers answered after surrender.* Every one of these is epistemologically identical to <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra's</Term> position: orientation toward the contents of <Term iast="sṛṣṭi">Sṛṣṭi</Term>, toward the psychological effects of devotion taken as primary evidence, toward social consensus as <Term iast="pramāṇa">pramāṇa</Term>. The category error is precise: these are psychological effects and social signals, not valid means of knowledge (<Term iast="pramāṇa">pramāṇa</Term>) for <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term>. <Term iast="vedānta">Vedānta</Term> does not deny that such experiences occur. It denies that they constitute evidence for what they claim to establish.

The cult mechanism operates by substituting these for the work of <Term iast="brahma vidyā">Brahma Vidyā</Term>. Social consensus, institutional authority, and miracle-verification are exactly what Kaṭha 1.2.23 excludes: they are *pravacana*, *medhā*, and *bahu-śruta* repackaged for a contemporary audience.

BG 7.19 speaks to the rarity of the prepared mind: <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="bg-7-19">*vāsudevaḥ sarvam iti sa mahātmā sudurlabhaḥ*</span>, "such a <Term iast="mahātman">mahātman</Term> who sees Vāsudeva as all is exceedingly rare." <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term>, which is everywhere, is recognized by almost no one, because the instrument of recognition, <Term iast="ātma vidyā">Ātma Vidyā</Term>, is almost never developed. The <Term iast="udyoga parva">Udyoga Parva's</Term> force lies not in warning but in diagnosis. <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> is given every instrument: <Term iast="sanatsujāta">Sanatsujāta's</Term> <Term iast="brahma vidyā">Brahma Vidyā</Term>, <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> living example, <Term iast="vidura">Vidura's</Term> practical wisdom, Krishna himself in the assembly. He refuses each one. The refusal proceeds from the deepest form of <Term iast="māyā">Māyā</Term>: the kind that presents itself as duty, love, and loyalty while remaining orientation toward the impermanent dressed as the permanent.

## Coda: Sañjaya as the Gītā's Epistemological Frame

The <Term iast="bhagavad gītā">Bhagavad Gītā</Term> begins with <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> narrating and ends with <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> summarizing. The framing is structural. <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term> is the <Term iast="gītā">Gītā's</Term> epistemological witness, the one whose recognized clarity makes the <Term iast="gītā">Gītā's</Term> testimony trustworthy within the <Term iast="mahābhārata">Mahābhārata's</Term> narrative architecture.

His statement at BG 18.76-77 that he rejoices again and again recollecting this dialogue (*hṛṣyāmi ca punaḥ punaḥ*) is a phenomenological report: the <Term iast="ānanda">ānanda</Term> that arises when <Term iast="jñāna">jñāna</Term> is intact and the seeing is clean. The <Term iast="gītā">Gītā's</Term> last verse through <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya</Term>:

> <span class="verse-ref" data-verse="bg-18-78">*yatra yogeśvaraḥ kṛṣṇo yatra pārtho dhanurdharaḥ / tatra śrīr vijayo bhūtir dhruvā nītir matir mama*</span> (BG 18.78)
> "Wherever Krishna the Lord of Yoga, wherever Pārtha the bowman: there, prosperity, victory, expansion, sound policy — this is my conviction."

A mind organized by <Term iast="ātma vidyā">Ātma Vidyā</Term> sees where the axis of reality runs and aligns with it. The verse is a consequence of <Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> cognitive architecture.

The <Term iast="udyoga parva">Udyoga Parva's</Term> diagnostic question is not "do you believe in <Term iast="brahman">Brahman</Term>?" It is: what is your actual orientation?

<Term iast="sañjaya">Sañjaya's</Term> answer to <Term iast="dhṛtarāṣṭra">Dhritarāṣṭra</Term> is a mirror.
